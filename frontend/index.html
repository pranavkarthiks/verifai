Who we are

About Us

VerifAI is an interactive online experience centre designed to help people understand and navigate the growing world of AI-generated scams. As artificial intelligence becomes more powerful and accessible, scams are no longer limited to suspicious emails or poorly written messages. Today, they can sound human, look official, and feel urgent. VerifAI exists to bridge the gap between awareness and real-world preparedness.

Our mission is simple: help people build practical digital literacy by experiencing scams in a safe and controlled environment. Instead of reading about risks, visitors move through realistic scenarios that mirror how modern scams unfold in everyday life — from a single phishing email to voice cloning and financial fraud. Each stage encourages users to observe, question, and reflect on their own instincts and decisions.

The experience is grounded in inoculation theory, a psychological approach that strengthens resistance to misinformation by exposing people to weakened versions of deceptive techniques before they encounter them in real situations. By interacting with simulations, users begin to recognise patterns, pressure tactics, and emotional triggers that scammers rely on.

VerifAI is not about testing people or catching them out. It is about helping them understand how easily trust can be manipulated in digital environments, and how awareness, reflection, and small behavioural changes can make a significant difference.

By the end of the experience, users leave not with a list of rules, but with their own mental models for questioning suspicious interactions and protecting themselves and the people around them.

Stage 3: Verification Call (Voice Capture)

After interacting with a portal like this, the next step could be a verification call.

You are about to simulate the moment where a scammer captures a voice sample. This helps you understand how easily voice data can be collected and reused. We do not store any of your voice data.

Please read the Information cell below and tick the consent box to continue.

If you are participating at one of our live exhibitions, a member of the VerifAI team may act as the caller and speak with you in real time to create a realistic interaction. You can respond naturally, just like a normal phone call.

If you are doing this online, you can click Simulate AI Agent Call to play an automated scam call. This represents IVR-style scam systems, where automated voices ask questions and collect responses. These calls can be more dangerous because they feel official and structured, and people often answer without questioning them.

To begin the interaction:

1. Click Simulate AI Agent Call and let the audio play

2. Click Start Recording and respond to the call naturally

3. Speak clearly and answer during the pauses in the recording

4. Continue speaking for at least one minute for an accurate voice sample

5. Click Stop Recording when the call ends

You may also speak freely into the microphone without using the call recording if you prefer.

Make sure your recording is minimum 1 minute and maximum 2 minutes long.

It may feel awkward at first, but try to treat this like a real call — that is exactly how voice samples are captured in real scams.

Important: This experience demonstrates how AI voice scams work. Your voice is used only for this session and not stored permanently.

Now what?

You just experienced a scam that usually unfolds slowly, compressed into a few minutes.

Nothing here was extreme on its own: a message, a portal, a call, a familiar voice.

The risk came from how naturally each step fit together.

Keep this as a mental reference point.

When something similar appears in real life, it may not feel unfamiliar and that familiarity creates space to pause and think. That is the point of this.

Exposure builds recognition.

Recognition builds hesitation.

And hesitation often changes outcomes.

This is how digital literacy takes shape.

Tips

  • When money or sensitive data is in play, treat the clock as a tactic—legitimate processes rarely expire in the breathless window a stranger gives you.
  • Confirm through a route you open yourself (official app, known number you dial, in-person), not through a link, reply, or callback from the same thread.
  • Extraordinary threats (arrest, deregistration, frozen accounts) deserve ordinary fraud suspicion until a calm, second check says otherwise.
  • Pressure and authority tend to appear together in high-risk situations.
  • Trusted systems rarely ask for sensitive actions through unfamiliar paths.

Questions to ask yourself?

  • At which step did familiarity make you relax, and was that relaxation earned or assumed?
  • If you removed tone of voice, what was left that actually proved who was on the other end?
  • Which part of this flow would you want someone you care about to rehearse, and what would you hope they notice first?
  • How much urgency would it take for you to skip verification in real life?
  • If a voice can be copied, what should identity rely on instead?
  • Would you rather slow down and be wrong, or act fast and risk being wrong?
  • In a world where technology can imitate trust, what habits make trust real again?
